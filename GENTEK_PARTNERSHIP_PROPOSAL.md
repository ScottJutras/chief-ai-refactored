## The Opportunity

Your contractors are your revenue. When they're profitable, they stay in business longer, take on more jobs, and buy more materials from you. When they're not — when they underbid, lose track of costs, or can't tell which jobs made money — they shrink, fail, or switch to cheaper suppliers to protect margins they can't even measure.

ChiefOS is a business operating system built specifically for contractors. It tracks every job's costs, revenue, labour, and materials in one place — and then explains the financial picture in plain language. No spreadsheets. No guesswork.

We'd like to integrate Gentek's product catalog directly into ChiefOS so that every contractor using our platform has instant access to your current product lines and pricing.

---

## What This Means for Gentek

### Your products become the default.

When a ChiefOS user builds a quote for a siding job, they pull materials from your catalog — your SKUs, your product names, your pricing. When they log an expense after visiting your distributor, they itemize against your catalog. Your products are embedded in their daily workflow.

This isn't advertising. It's integration. Switching away from Gentek means changing the tool they use every day.

### Your contractors become more profitable.

A contractor who understands their job profitability makes better decisions — better pricing, better material planning, less waste. ChiefOS gives them that understanding. More profitable contractors stay in business longer and buy more materials.

### You get market intelligence.

With contractor consent, we can share anonymized, aggregated data: which product categories are most quoted, seasonal demand patterns, regional pricing sensitivity. This is the kind of purchasing intelligence that typically requires expensive market research.

### Your brand is associated with innovation.

Being the first building products manufacturer integrated into an AI-native contractor operating system positions Gentek as a forward-thinking partner — not just a supplier.

---

## What We Need from Gentek

### 1. Your product catalog in spreadsheet format.

A standard Excel file with your current product lines — SKU, product name, description, category, unit of measure, and list pricing. This is likely something you already have internally.

### 2. Quarterly pricing updates.

When pricing changes, send us an updated spreadsheet. We handle the rest — parsing, validation, and updating the catalog in our system. We'll send a friendly reminder when updates are due.

### 3. A point of contact.

One person at Gentek who can answer questions about product categories, pricing tiers, and catalog format when we're setting up the integration.

---

## What We Are NOT Asking For

- No revenue share. This is a co-marketing partnership.
- No API development. A spreadsheet is all we need.
- No exclusive arrangement. We will integrate other suppliers too — but Gentek would be first.
- No financial commitment. This costs Gentek nothing except the time to send a spreadsheet.

## How It Works for the Contractor

### Scenario 1: Building a Quote

The contractor is preparing a quote for a vinyl siding job. They tell Chief: "Build me a quote for the Henderson siding job — 30 squares of premium vinyl, J-channel, and starter strip."

Chief pulls from Gentek's current catalog, calculates materials cost at current pricing, and builds a professional quote document — with Gentek's product names and SKUs — that the contractor sends to their client.

### Scenario 2: Logging Materials

The contractor just left the supply house with $1,400 in materials. They text Chief: "Spent $1,400 at Gentek for the Henderson job." Chief offers to itemize against Gentek's catalog, breaking the expense into line items with unit pricing. Now the job cost breakdown shows exactly what materials went where — not just a lump sum.

### Scenario 3: Job Profitability

At the end of the job, the contractor asks: "Did the Henderson job make money?" Chief shows revenue versus costs, broken down by materials (with Gentek product details), labour, and other expenses. The contractor sees exactly which material costs were higher than quoted and adjusts future pricing.
